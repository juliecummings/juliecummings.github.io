Arduino Assignments

Arduino is an open-source electronics platform based on easy-to-use hardware and software. It is intended for artists, designers and anyone interested in creating interactive objects or environments. Arduino consists of an open source micro-controller and a software environment to program it in. Arduino projects can run stand-alone (without a computer), but can also communicate with software that runs on a computer (eg Processing, MaxMSP). Arduino boards are able to read inputs — light on a sensor, a finger on a button, or a Twitter message — and turn it into an output — activating a motor, turning on an LED, publishing something online.

EXERCISE 4C
Exercise 4 asks that I make one LED gradually light up whilst the other gradually dims down. This exercise brings forth the use of the analogWrite() function which allows me to set the brightness of an LED from 0 (completely off) to 255 (completely on) or anywhere in between. In order for an LED to slowly fade from 0 to 255, a for loop is utilized. In the process of figuring exercise 4c out, I hit a roadbump when I tried to implement a second variable that decreases as opposed to increases into my for loop. Whilst it worked physical for this purpose, I was told that as a general rule of thumb, one does not do such a thing. Still curious about my mistake, I searched the why not of it all after the workshop. As it turns out, it complicates the parameters set within the for loop and wires (variables) will inevitably get criss-crossed. As such, I further discovered that a nested for loop was a possible solution for such a predicament. Irregardless, a simpler solution for the current exercise at hand was to simply take the dewindling LED as the inverse of the illuminating LED by substracting the brightness variable from 255 (the maximum brightness) as seen in the provided code shown below.

EXERCISE 6C
After previously playing around with using a potmeter value as an analog input by viewing it on the serial monitor and serial plotter, exercise 6 is about controlling an LED with a potmeter. More specifically, making an LED dim or illuminate gradually using a potmeter. To make one LED fade in while the other fades out and vice versa, the analogWrite() function used are similar to that of exercise 4. The difference being that in order to use the analog input received by the potmeter which is on a scale of 0 to 1023 to control the LEDs, the value must first be scaled down to the value of 0 to 255 of which an LED is controlled by. Due to this scaling, the variable sensorValue must be changed to a float as opposed to an integer. The sensor value can be manually scaled down or the map() function can be incorporated. The syntax of this function is map(value, fromLow, fromHigh, toLow, toHigh) where 'value' is the number to map which in this case is the sensorValue, 'fromLow' is the lower bound of the value’s current range, 'fromHigh' is the upper bound of the value’s current range, 'toLow' is the lower bound of the value’s target range and 'toHigh' is the upper bound of the value’s target range.

EXERCISE 7
Exercise 7 aims to control the LEDs with light using a Light Dependent Resistor (LDR). An LDR is another kind of sensor and it is a sensitive resistor that measures the amount of light which works like the sensors used to turn street lights on when it gets dark outside. The objective is to make the LEDs fade according to the amount of light that the LDR perceives. Just like with the potmeter, the LDR sensor value, under ideal circumstances, ranges between 0 to 1023. However, the actual range the sensor reaches under my personal lighting conditions would likely be a lot smaller to account for less than optimal variables. To obtain the measured range in practice, I noted down the highest (unobscured) and lowest (obscured) value gathered through the serial monitor. I then used the map() function to define the actual range and to rescale it to the 0-255 range the LEDs accept. Subsequently, I was greeted by blinking LEDs that happened as a result of the sensor value being out of range. As it turns out, the light will completely turn on when the sensor value is a negative number, causing the unanticipated flickering. In order to remedy this, I incorporated the constrain() function to make sure the number is never lower than 0. All functions used (and more) with their accompanying syntax and parameters can be further read up on in the reference on Arduino's site.

EXERCISE 8
Exercise 8 plays around with the notion of a voltage divider. A voltage divider is a circuit that turns a large input voltage (maximum 5V with the Arduino Uno used) into a smaller output one. Using two resistors and an input voltage, we can create an output voltage that is a fraction of the input. In this exercise, variable resistors, namely Light Dependant Resistors, are used instead of pre-determined ones. When placed in the dark, the resistor should be quite high (approximately 5MΩ or 5000000Ω) and when placed in bright light, it should be quite low (approximately 200Ω). Of course, these numericals are derived with the assumption of ideal circumstances.

The theory is that based off of knowing the input voltage and the resistance range (in the dark and in bright light), I will be able to use the formula below to calculate output voltage, which will then be reflected in the brightness of the LEDs. Theoretically, there are 4 test cases that can be verify this voltage divider / reduction notion by using said formula.



TEST CASE 1 — LDR1 is obscured (in the dark) and the LDR2 is unobscured (in bright light).
In such a scenario, the reduced output voltage should be approximately 0.0002 V, as proven in the calculations above. The lower voltage should result in dimmer LEDs.

TEST CASE 2 — LDR1 is unobscured (in bright light) and the LDR2 is obscured (in the dark).
In this scenario, the output voltage should reflect 4.99V based on the following calculated estimations. This should bring about brighter LEDs.

TEST CASE 3 — LDR1 and LDR2 are both obscured (in the dark).
From calculations made off of the given formula, the output voltage should be 2,5V. This is also consistent with the implication that two resistors of the same value would result in an output voltage that is half of the input voltage.

TEST CASE 4 — LDR1 and LDR2 are both unobscured (in bright light).
Just like with test case 3, the output voltage should be 2,5V which once again supports that two resistors of the same value would result in an output voltage that is half of the input voltage.

To test out this theory in practice, I first watch the brightness of the LEDs as the aforementioned test cases were set in motion. Once I was remotely satisfied with the results, I further supported my assumptions through the serial monitor which was scaled down to fit the voltage scale (0-5). With test case 2, it was recorded at around 4.7V. As of test cases 3 and 4, output voltage for both were monitored to be roughly between the range of 2.4V to 2.6V. All of which is in sync with my previously stated theory-based predictions. The slight discrepancies can be accounted for by factoring in that the conditions are sub-optimal at best.

CLOSE PROCESS

Product

This is a showcase of the look and functionality of our final product presented at the EXPO. It also touches on the intermediate products and subsidiary functionalities leading up to this. A process was taken to eventually get to this product and a more evaluative reflection of the product, with its contributing factors, is also complementary to the product itself.

— ★ —

THE PRODUCT PRESENTED AT THE EXPO
Our product, which we named as Nom-Nom, is intended to facilitate recycling. NOM NOM is a smart recycling bin. It has the appearance of a plant in order to remind users about how important recycling is for our environment. The system we have developed strives to motivate users to interact with it through displaying emotions on a LED matrix. The plant detects the nearest person and follows it with its head in an effort to seek attention. If you contain your smile, she expresses her disappointment through her eyes. If you smile, the plant smiles back with its eyes and open its jaw. Once the jaw is open, users are able to throw bottle caps and can tabs into its mouth for recycling. After a pre-determined time, she will close her mouth once again and the idea carries on. While we had all the parts to bring this to fruition, our head structure and lack of long wires resulted in multiple omissions.



Unfortunately, what we were able to bring forth during the EXPO was Nom-Nom who still follows a face she detects but without her expressive eyes and always having her mouth ajar for bottle caps and can tabs to be thrown in as well as a collection tray located in her pot.



While we were elated that our product proved sturdy and durable by lasting through the 4-hour long EXPO with minimal implications to its structure and functionality, there were complications with the program when there were too many people in its eyeline. This reflects on our face detection program but also with the presentation and layout of our installation. Plus, through a reoccurance with a number of presentations to visitors, I realized that users liked seeing what Nom-Nom sees and the throwing aspect should not be neglected. A speculation with the second preference is that people probably liked tactile qualities, active participation or the idea of being in control of something.


INTERMEDIATE PRODUCTS
First intermediate product we created was emotions illustrated on a pixel grid. From this, we found out that black could not be effectively used as a colour because that would just mean the LED is switched off. So, designs should preferably contain no black outlines. Subsequently, we made animated illustrations to test its possibility. We wanted to try this out as movement attracts attention and draws one's eyes. Additionally, it would make Nom-Nom look and feel more alive. We then created emotions projected onto the RGB LED 16 by 32 matrix. We put colour and animations on hold to prioritize smoothing out technical link between (closest) smile detection and emotions.



In terms of structural in-betweens, we made a paper mache over a balloon for the head, which then got replaced by two semi-sphere metal netted covers. Additionally, we intended to using a two-tier desk organizer within the pot for the collection tray and for hiding the hardware. However, due to the slight alteration of form when the clay formed, we improvised retaining the existing frame placed vertically and only keeping one tray for the collection.




POSSIBLE VALUE AND RELEVANCE OF THE PRODUCT
Aesthetically, this beats any regular old recycling bin. It subtly engages the user to recycle. This is of value as recycling is becoming a pressing concern for society. Plus, an end user would need a collection point for their recyclables. As such, we incorporated a collection tray to depict how the pot can be used for storage. This product is relevant as a basis for an alternative recycling bin. It can be refined and developed to lower production costs or provide more interactions.

CLOSE PRODUCT

Reflection

Here is my reflection of the entirety of the process (group and experience-wise) as well as the various facets surrounding the product. As this is a reflection, the views expressed by me may not be universal but are well-founded in my experience through the 9 days of Lab Weeks.

— ★ —

THE PRODUCT
With regards to the product itself, I was content with it for the first few days. When I saw that we have not achieved any intermediary products / checkpoints, I was worried about our concepts' feasilbility. However, everyone else seemed determined on this one track path and I left it be. I should have pushed harder. Additionally, I felt that the technological aspect and the design + building of the product were kept separated, which was not ideal for the viability of the product. I found that this arose due to a lack of communication and democracy (involving everyone in decision-making), resulting in costs incurred that were on the high side. As a whole, I was fond of the overall concept but I just discerned that its execution could have been refined as it was a tremendous shame that a multitude of aspects contributing to our concept did not come together in the final product. The intermediate technologies made to work were remarkably satisfying! However, as previously mentioned, it should have went hand in hand and worked closely with a mastermind of the construction of form.

Personally, recycling would not have been my first choice but it is an ever-growing pressing issue and as such, a worthy cause to tackle. Plus, I felt that we had sufficiently addressed how our product would support recycling in theory. On the contrary, I observed that the fun aspect, as spelled out in our promise, was not delivered to fruition. I gathered that this is due to our concept barely evolving as we went along. We started with wanting to create something spunky as well as feisty and did not adapt it to the new playfulness we wish to incorporate and achieve. On the structural front, we did not pay close enough attention to the head and what it required to make it open and close. Plus, the weight load our head could support should have been tested earlier as it was the factor that forced us into numerous omissions. Last but not least, the connection between head to stem could have been better executed.

All in all, our product did withstand four hours of usage with minimal complications to its structure and functionality. So, I am incredibly proud of its sturdiness and durability. With regards to the technology used though, it had to be reloaded every so often to prevent hiccups. At some point during the EXPO, there was poor planning when the laptop slept and as I was not the owner, I could not unlock it. Also, it gets easily confused when there are multiple people in its eyeline. which reflects more on the setup we had. We should have barred it better so it is more exclusive. More on the nitty-gritty side of things, the can tabs and bottle caps were mostly neglected as it is not visible but rather, placed by the wall, to a side and on the floor (out of view, out of mind). As such we could have definitely put in more thought into the presentation and the layout of our installation (placements and all), which was not done owing to poor time management. However, the genuine response and interest shown by visitors and schoolmates during the EXPO did make me consider if I am too harsh on my judgement of our product.


THE GROUP
On the group collaboration front, I noted that all of us were mostly passive and no one stepped up to the leadership mantle. There were no distinct roles or task allocation (everyone is just doing whatever they please) as time went by. Plus, tasks were not assigned based on strengths which could definitely have improved our speed and efficiency. Group cooperation wise, there were prominent issues with the language barrier and self justified decisions as mentioned in the process. There was a clear lack of communication, which was progressively notable and a deterrence to the effectiveness of the team. Numerous sole or duo decision-making moments resulted in a subpar ability to build of the ideas of others, which was the beauty of group creation. While I did cherish the creation aspect of this process, I wished I could have some input in programming as I wish to grow in that skill. I offered but was declined by our programmer and I respected his expertise. In hindsight, I should not have undermine my own capabilities and insisted more. Even though spontaneity can be promising in provoking creativity, I found the level of spontaneity to be too high. The constant "do first, think later" mentality of each individual was not kind to our pockets or time crunch. In the second week, there was little to no forward thinking. Discussion and planning became non-existent, which is counter-productive to our diminishing time constrain. Finally, what I found most baffling is the unwillingness to share resources. It made me feel like a pariah and unwanted at times. For instance, I offered and repeated myself thrice in our group whatsapp, and once individually, about the group video and how I was willing to comply the video clips we all made. I was ignored and did not get a single response or a single picture or video from anyone. Being made to feel invisible was extraordinarily depressing and while I know that I should retain my work ethics and push through, I admit that, in all honesty, it negatively affected the effort I put into complying my own pictures and videos.


THE EXPERIENCE
LAB WEEKS: On top of the intensive process, back to back days and long hours, the 3-hour per day travelling time and accompanying hefty costs were truly weighing me down with each passing day. It might have been reflected on my enthusiasm levels over time but I did not let it affect the quantity and quality of my work. While I am sufficiently equipped in group cooperation, I discerned that I still have much to work on when it comes to group collaboration. On my personal development with group work, I perceived that I desperately need to learn how to be heard by possibly being more assertive. Developing my people skills (being likeable) could be beneficial to the aforementioned. However, I am aware that this cannot realistically be improved overnight but should definitely be worked on diligently from now on. The process was nail-biting and kept me on the edge of my seat. It was an experience worth cherishing. Plus, to briefly check in on the processes of other groups was also an intriguing experience to behold. On the other hand, I identified that there was a disparity between working conventions as I was taught to be willing to change course whenever necessary ("do not get too attached to your first ideas" mentality) while I noted that the others in my group were determined to have a one-path process and mould it to succession. It was an unorthodox approach for me but I admire the determination. In summary, I enjoyed the experience and it taught me to have more confidence in myself as well as others and be less critical. Creation can and should be fun!

EXPO: The EXPO was indeed an exciting and crazy experience to have. Taking the time to look at what everyone achieved was enlightening. What I personally noticed is the installations that stuck in my mind tend to be the simplest of concepts that were developed and grown upon through the days. Examples include Packzy, FeedMe, Lemon Maze and You & Eye, to name a few. By presenting at the EXPO, I also got a better grasp at the inner workings of presentations — how to draw visitors in as well as how to retain their attention by being more engaging in pitching the concept. The EXPO also presented an opportunity to present to a larger number of audience than I am accustomed to. This first-hand encounter reinforces in me, the need for user testing because of all the valuable insights I received and gathered. First and foremost, I was thinking mostly in the technical aspect but visitors were drawn in by the aesthetics and were much easily impressed with the technology than I expected. Plus, the reviews were motivating and way better than I anticipated. I was expecting the worse, to say the least. On top of all this, I observed, through a number of presentations I gave, that users liked seeing what Nom-Nom sees and the throwing aspect should not be neglected. Even though the throwing of bottle caps was not as elaborate as we envisioned, the audience enjoyed the interactivity anyway. Through the proceedings of the EXPO, my biggest takeaway is that through hardwork and preserverance, we can build unbelievably awesome products in just 9 days because all of us (hundred-plus of us) did so!

WERE EXPECTATIONS MET? While I was doubtful of its necessity at the start, the location was a good call for this purpose (away from school + own spot). Maakhaven was home for 9 days and created the mentality of free minds (encourages idea generation), productive tinkering and a can-do spirit. However, in terms of my pre-conceived expectations, I am a tad disappointed that I did not really touched any programming (except for about an hour looking into the LED matrix) as I anticipated the chance to further practise and improve my programming capabilities. I imagine I could have if I had insisted with more fervour but was hesitant as I did not want to step on any toes. Regardless, I also expected this course of activities to be different and challenging and it did held up on that end of the bargain. The extent of the lab weeks has indeed instilled a sense of accomplishment, ownership and satisfaction + renewed confidence that comes with it. I absolutely enjoyed the conceptualization and its creation. In the end, I was proud of what we achieved. I most certainly would not have imagined I was capable of accomplishing all this if you had asked me two weeks before. Who knows what I can create two years from now?
